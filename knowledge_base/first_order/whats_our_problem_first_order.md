About the book
From the mind behind the acclaimed blog Wait But Why, Tim
Urban invites readers on an engaging exploration of the
perplexities of our modern existence in his book What's Our
Problem? After gaining fame through insightful and often
humorous essays on topics ranging from AI to procrastination,
Urban shifts gears to examine the chaotic social landscape of
recent years. Delving into history, evolutionary psychology,
and political theory, he seeks to unpack the reasons behind our
increasingly tribal behaviors and societal disarray. This
expansive work introduces a groundbreaking framework for
understanding contemporary challenges, moving beyond
traditional political divides to focus on the ways we think as
individuals and groups. With Urban's signature wit, original
concepts, and over 300 illustrations, readers embark on a
thought-provoking journey that promises to transform their
perspective on the world.

About the author

his engaging, thought-provoking exploration of complex ideas
through the lens of his popular blog, Wait But Why. With a

humor, deep research, and illustrations. His work often delves

human behavior, making complex topics accessible to a broad
audience. In his book "What's Our Problem?", Urban tackles
some of the most pressing issues facing humanity today,
inviting readers to reflect on the collective dilemmas of
modern life with his signature wit and clarity.

Chapter 1 Summary : The Ladder
CHAPTER 1: The Ladder
The Tug-of-War in Our Heads
  
Humans possess a complex nature, shaped by genetics that
strive for survival and reproduction. The Primitive Mind
governs basic survival instincts, while the Higher Mind
enables self-reflection and insight. The tension between these
two minds results in various internal conflicts, leading to
differing approaches to beliefs and behaviors.
On Multiple Minds
  

Philosophers and psychologists have explored the concept of
“multiple minds” for centuries, proposing different structures
that describe human thought processes. This chapter
introduces the idea of a psychological Ladder with four
rungs, reflecting states of thinking behavior that can
influence beliefs.
Vertical Thinking
  
Beliefs significantly shape our perception and behavior.
Understanding how beliefs are formed involves examining
both the Idea Spectrum (what we think) and the Ladder (how
we think). The Higher Mind seeks truth and adaptability in
beliefs, while the Primitive Mind clings to existing
convictions, leading to misunderstandings and biases.
The Four Rungs of the Ladder
  
1. 
Thinking Like a Scientist
: This top rung prioritizes truth-seeking, learning, and
open-mindedness. It is characterized by hypothesis
formation, evidence evaluation, and rigorous testing.
  

2. 
Thinking Like a Sports Fan
: Enthusiasm for desired outcomes can distort reasoning,
leading to confirmation bias and motivated reasoning. This
rung reflects a desire for validation while still holding some
willingness to change views.
3. 
Thinking Like an Attorney
: An attorney's mindset is focused on winning arguments
rather than seeking truth, often cherry-picking evidence to
support predetermined beliefs. This leads to a more rigid
form of thinking, where ideas become fixed and
unresponsive to evidence.
4. 
Thinking Like a Zealot
: At this lowest rung, beliefs are held with total conviction,
defensive of identity and status. Zealots exhibit an “us vs.
them” mentality, resisting any challenges to their beliefs.
High-Rung Thinking vs. Low-Rung Thinking
  
High-rung thinking promotes learning and adaptability, while
low-rung thinking reinforces ignorance and rigidity. The
balance between these two forms shapes individual and

societal outcomes.
Intellectual Cultures
  
Cultural environments impact how individuals think. Idea
Labs encourage collaborative high-rung thinking, fostering
open discourse, while Echo Chambers promote conformity
and low-rung thinking, stifling innovation and improvement.
The influence of these cultures can significantly affect
individual psyches.
Emergence
  
Humans display emergent behavior—uniting to form
collaborative groups such as communities or organizations.
This can lead to constructive systems (genies) that enhance
shared knowledge or destructive systems (golems) driven by
conformity and conflict. 
The Liberal Games vs. Power Games
  
The chapter discusses the historical transition from the Power
Games of survival to the Liberal Games, characterized by
principles of freedom, safety, and equality. This new

framework aimed to promote cooperation, individual rights,
and progress rather than oppressive power dynamics.
The Challenge of the Human Condition
  
While the Liberal Games strive to improve societal
structures, the underlying Primitive Mind continues to exert
influence, leading to a tension in human nature. The chapter
concludes with the understanding that navigating these
dynamics is crucial for our progress as individuals and as a
society.

Example
Key Point:Understanding the duality of the Primitive
Mind and Higher Mind is essential for personal
growth.
Example:Imagine waking up one morning with a
pressing decision to make about a new job opportunity.
Your Primitive Mind insists on clinging to the comfort
of your current situation, warning you of risk and
uncertainty, while your Higher Mind encourages you to
analyze the potential for growth and learning in the new
role. This internal tug-of-war illustrates the constant
battle between survival instincts and the pursuit of truth,
highlighting how this dynamic affects your beliefs and
choices.

Chapter 2 Summary: Politics on the Ladder
Introduction to Political Dimensions
- The chapter begins by framing the political landscape in the
U.S. as a binary system of conservatives (Republican) and
progressives (Democratic). This oversimplification overlooks
the nuanced ideologies within each party.
- Terms "liberal" and "progressive" are clarified: "liberal"
refers to a broader Enlightenment philosophy, whereas
"progressive" specifically indicates left-leaning politics.
Introducing the Ladder Framework
- The author proposes a vertical dimension to politics,
represented by a "Ladder," which distinguishes between
high-rung (thoughtful, analytical) and low-rung (tribal,
simplistic) political thinking. 

- High-rung thinkers focus on the pursuit of truth, engage in
critique, and exhibit humility. Conversely, low-rung thinking
is characterized by binary perceptions and tribalism.
Aspects of High-Rung Politics
- High-rung politics encourages open-ended debates
structured around three critical questions:
  1. What Is? - Understanding current realities through
political science and history.
  2. What Should Be? - Philosophical discussions on ideal
solutions and ethical implications.
  3. How Do We Get There? - Exploring strategies to bridge
the gap between current and ideal states.
The Duality of Political Engagement
- High-rung thinkers often oscillate between progressive and
conservative stances, allowing for nuanced debates.
- The interplay between progressivism (pushing for change)
and conservatism (preserving beneficial aspects) is
acknowledged as vital in political discourse.
Spectrum Battles and Compromise

- Some political disagreements focus on finding where to
position policies on a spectrum rather than whether to
change. This ongoing negotiation helps refine policies over
time.
Challenges of Low-Rung Politics
- Low-rung politics resembles a simplified narrative similar
to Disney movies, casting political opponents as purely good
or evil.
- This oversimplification is harmful, fostering division and
diminishing empathy for political opponents.
Characteristics of Low-Rung Thinking and Culture
- Low-rung participants adopt tribalism, leading to rigid
narratives that justify hypocrisy and inconsistency in
principles.
- The "golem" classification is introduced for individuals
trapped in low-rung political environments, showing
conformity to group narratives and rejecting dissent.
Golem Immune System Mechanisms

- Golems maintain belief systems through filtering
(controlling information), twisting (manipulating facts), and
confirmation bias (reinforcing groupthink).
- Each mechanism serves to protect the narrative while
stifling critical thinking and debate.
High-Rung Immune System
- Conversely, high-rung politics values open discourse and
promotes the critical analysis of information, leading to more
informed political engagement.
- The high-rung immune system relies on a culture of debate
and moral consistency to shield against low-rung descent.
Conclusion: The Tug-of-War in Politics
- U.S. politics can be visualized as an ecosystem, balanced
between high-rung progress and low-rung tribalism. History
suggests that democratic systems are constructed to
withstand low-rung challenges, but recent societal changes
risk destabilizing this balance.
- Recognizing these dynamics is crucial for addressing
contemporary political issues.

Example
Key Point:Understanding Political Engagement
Example:Imagine you find yourself in a heated
discussion about a recent government policy with
friends. As the debate intensifies, you notice some
participants relying solely on their partisan beliefs,
dismissing any evidence that contradicts their views. In
this scenario, your ability to step back and ask, 'What is
the reality behind this policy?', 'What should it ideally
accomplish?' and 'What steps will lead us there?'
exemplifies high-rung political thinking. By prioritizing
critical questions over tribal narratives, you cultivate
greater empathy and seek to engage in a constructive
conversation rather than a polarizing argument. This
approach not only deepens your understanding but also
enriches the collective dialogue among your peers.

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The binary view of political ideologies
may simplify complex realities.
Critical Interpretation:Urban's ladder framework offers a
compelling lens to understand political engagement, yet
it risks reinforcing a dichotomy rather than embracing
the full spectrum of ideological beliefs. By framing
politics strictly through the lens of high-rung versus
low-rung thinkers, Urban invites readers to consider a
simplistic categorization of issues that often demands a
nuanced perspective. Critics argue that such binary
classifications can perpetuate divisive narratives,
potentially alienating those who navigate the political
landscape in more complex ways. A study on political
polarization published in the American Political Science
Review highlights how oversimplified political
identities can hinder compromise and encourage
polarization (Abramowitz & Saunders, 2008). Thus,
while Urban's framework is a robust starting point, it
may not capture the intricacies inherent in political
beliefs and behaviors. Readers should therefore
approach this concept with caution and seek to engage
with the multifaceted nature of political ideologies.

Chapter 3 Summary : The Downward

CHAPTER 3: The Downward Spiral
Overview of American Political Climate
- The essence of the chapter revolves around the notion that
something is fundamentally wrong in America, often
attributed to rising political tribalism and authoritarianism,
which Urban metaphorically describes as “golems” gaining
strength.
  
Shift 1: From Distributed Tribalism to Concentrated
Tribalism
- The chapter outlines historical political conflicts in
America, illustrating that while the 1950s appeared
politically unified, substantial underlying divisions existed.
Urban emphasizes how a common enemy unites factions,

which leads to a temporary resolution of inter-group
conflicts.
- As the external threats dissipated (e.g., the Cold War),
internal political divisions intensified. Particularly, the
ideological realignment of political parties during the Civil
Rights Movement created a concentrated divide, sidelining
moderate voices and culminating in a more rigid party
affiliation among voters.
Shift 2: From Concentrated Tribalism to
Hypercharged Tribalism
- Urban identifies geographic sorting as a consequence of
increasing mobility and education, where people choose to
live among like-minded individuals, leading to cultural and
ideological homogeneity.
- The chapter discusses the evolution of media from
broad-based broadcasting to narrowcasting, highlighting how
the media environment caters to specific political tribes,
reinforcing existing biases and promoting extreme views.
- The role of social media algorithms is examined and
criticized for trapping users in echo chambers, further
extreme views.

Chapter 4 Summary : Rise of the Red
Golem
Chapter 4: Rise of the Red Golem
Background and Political Landscape
- Growing up in Massachusetts, the author views the political
divide as simplistic, characterized by the "Obviously Good
Party" (Democrats) and the "Obviously Bad Party"
(Republicans).
- Political affiliations and opinions evolve with time, yet a
strong aversion to the Republican Party remains due to
controversial past actions (e.g., the Iraq War, selection of
Sarah Palin as VP, climate change denial).
Republican Party Dynamics in the 1960s
- The Republican Party in the early 1960s had moderate
leaders like Dwight Eisenhower, but more rigid and extreme
factions, termed the Republican Fundamentalists (RFs),

began to rise in power.
- The RFs differentiated themselves through their low-rung
mentality and extremist views, rejecting complexity and
compromise, leading to increasing internal party tensions.
Low-Rung Thinking and Tactics
- RFs emphasized binary thinking, creating a clear good
versus evil dichotomy while displaying a notable opposition
to the civil rights movement.
- Their strategies included disruptively taking control of
organizations (like the Young Republicans) and amplifying
their extremist views while sidelining moderate voices.
Goldwater's Campaign and Shift in Party Identity
- Barry Goldwater's 1964 presidential campaign embodied
low-rung qualities, advocating for extreme beliefs and
appealing to segregationist Democrats.
- Despite their loss in the election, Goldwater's candidacy
firmly established the RFs’ influence within the party,
emphasizing a cult of extremism over mainstream
conservatism.

Evolution in the GOP and Reflection on High-Rung
Politics
- The post-Goldwater GOP saw a pivot towards a more
ideologically consistent party under Ronald Reagan, who
was seen as both high-rung and a master storyteller,
contributing to the consolidation of power and coherent party
identity.
- Over time, the Republican Party transitioned from
Reagan's, who sought unity, to more divisive figures like
Newt Gingrich, who emphasized a culture of hostility
towards Democrats.
The Rise of Trump and the Transformation of the
GOP
- Trump’s rise marked a definitive shift from Reagan’s
vision, embodying a politics of grievance and division, and
confirming what many viewed as the party's evolution
towards low-rung tactics.
- The behavior of the GOP post-2016 began reflecting a
willingness to engage in anti-democratic practices and reject
basic democratic norms, culminating in events like the
January 6 Capitol riot.

Conclusion: The Current State of the GOP and the
Political Landscape
- The GOP under Trump represents a radical departure from
its historical principles, embodying low-rung characteristics
that have persisted and flourished in the current political
climate.
- The chapter warns of the broader implications for American
democracy; a political golem, symbolizing low-rung politics,
is succeeding, reflecting a decline in high-rung reasoning and
collaboration. 
Reflection on Personal Political Understanding
- The author expresses recognition that the issues confronting
political discourse stem not just from conservatism, but a
broader loss of high-rung engagement across the political
spectrum, emphasizing that all sides are affected by the
low-rung dynamics at play.

Chapter 5 Summary : Social Justice,
High and Low
CHAPTER 5: Social Justice, High and Low
Introduction to Wokeness
The rise of the wokeness movement in America since 2014
has created significant cultural and political rifts. Discussions
have evolved from topics like identity politics to specific
social justice issues such as Black Lives Matter, online
censorship, and transgender rights.
Two Kinds of Social Justice
1. 
Liberal Social Justice (LSJ)
: LSJ activists perceive America through two narratives. The
first is an aspirational story of equality founded on
Enlightenment values, while the second reveals historical
contradictions where these ideals have been betrayed,

particularly regarding racial and gender equality. LSJ seeks
to hold society accountable to its promises of freedom and
justice.
   
2. 
Social Justice Fundamentalism (SJF)
: In contrast, SJF has emerged as a more radical ideology
rooted in neo-Marxism and Critical Theory. It suggests that
liberalism is flawed and that systemic oppression is
pervasive, advocating for a more extensive overhaul of
societal structures to dismantle ingrained inequalities.
Marxism and Its Evolution
SJF, influenced by the Frankfurt School and concepts like
identity politics and Critical Theory, emphasizes
intersectionality, which analyzes how various forms of
identity converge to create unique experiences of oppression.
The Concept of Privilege
The concept of privilege, introduced by Peggy McIntosh,
highlights unacknowledged advantages granted to certain
groups, particularly white people. This notion directly

connects to SJF’s framework of understanding systemic
racism and oppression.
Intersectionality and Postmodernism
Intersectionality materializes from the work of Kimberlé
Crenshaw, connecting various marginalized identities.
Postmodernism, emerging in the 1960s, critiques the notion
of objective truth, adding complexity to SJF and enabling a
further skepticism of established narratives.
The Fusion of SJF and Postmodernism
This blending results in Social Justice Fundamentalism, a
radical approach that reframes issues of race, gender, and
power dynamics as systemic, viewing them through a
singular lens of oppression while rejecting liberal solutions
as inadequate.
SJF vs. LSJ
SJF profoundly diverges from LSJ by viewing societal
dynamics as primarily a power struggle. It reacts to
disparities through punitive measures like affirmative action,

equating equality of opportunity with equality of outcome,
which often leads to drastic solutions that may contradict
liberal values.
Critiques of SJF
1. 
Unscientific Nature
: SJF often operates on a dogmatic belief system lacking
empirical validation, reducing complex issues to binary
categories.
   
2. 
Echo Chambers
: The SJF community enforces conformity and silences
dissenting views, resulting in narrow narratives that
discourage critical discourse and exploration of diverse
perspectives.
3. 
Moral Inconsistency
: Double standards exist in how marginalized and privileged
groups are treated within SJF narratives, leading to a paradox
of tolerance where oppressors face active criticism while
oppressed groups receive leniency for harmful attitudes.

Censorship as a Tool of Control
The chapter underscores the dangers of censorship in a
society, where limiting free speech fosters a disconnected
and indoctrinated population—the "golem"—that cannot
think critically.
The Importance of Free Speech
Free speech serves as the backbone of a healthy democracy,
enabling diverse ideas to flourish and allowing marginalized
voices to be heard. An open marketplace of ideas is essential
for societal progress and moral growth.
Conclusion
Social Justice Fundamentalism presents challenges to liberal
values by promoting a rigid system of beliefs that often
disregards the importance of free speech and the complexity
of societal dynamics. The future discourse around social
justice will need to navigate these tensions to foster
understanding and meaningful change in a fragmented
society.

Critical Thinking
Key Point:Censorship stifles critical thought and
controls discourse in society.
Critical Interpretation:Tim Urban highlights that
censorship, prevalent in the Social Justice
Fundamentalism narrative, serves as a detrimental force
on public discourse. While the author emphasizes the
dangers of limiting free speech, one must critically
examine whether Urban's portrayal of censorship fully
encapsulates the complexities of the social justice
movement. For instance, some argue that censorship can
sometimes be justified in protecting vulnerable groups
from harmful rhetoric, showing that absolute freedom of
speech might not always align with the ideals of
equality and justice. This perspective is supported by
scholars like John Stuart Mill, who, while advocating
for free speech, also acknowledged the need for limiting
it to prevent harm. Therefore, readers should consider
that while Urban argues for unfettered discourse as vital
for democracy, the reality might reflect a more nuanced
balance between protecting individuals and fostering
open dialogue.

Concept of Idea Supremacy

authoritarianism, suppressing dissent in environments meant

Chapter 7: How to Conquer a Society
  
Introduction
  
This chapter discusses the rise of Social Justice

Fundamentalism (SJF) and its implications for society,
particularly focusing on the concept of "cancel culture."
Understanding Cancel Culture
  
"Cancel culture" has become a significant term for describing
various societal changes and conversations surrounding
social justice. However, Urban argues for a more nuanced
understanding, introducing the concept of the "illiberal
staircase" as a framework to understand different levels of
SJF incidents that reflect a decline in liberal principles.
Illiberal Staircase
  
The staircase is divided into steps that represent increasing
levels of illiberal behavior, starting with "Speech Control" at
Step 1. Urban discusses notable incidents involving
individuals like Donald McNeil and Alexi McCammond,
highlighting how accusations of past behavior resulted in
professional repercussions, often steeped in a lack of context
and understanding of intent.
Examples of Speech Control
  

McNeil's case at The New York Times illustrates how
offensive remarks can lead to severe professional
repercussions, misinterpretations of intent, and how
discourse surrounding racism has evolved over the years.
McCammond's termination from Teen Vogue reflects similar
sentiments of zero tolerance for past social media posts.
These cases are framed within the context of how society has
increasingly favored extreme reactions over measured
responses.
Forced Listening and Training
  
Moving down to Step 2, the chapter addresses the rise of
mandatory trainings in companies and schools that push SJF
ideologies. Employees are compelled to participate in these
trainings to avoid backlash, and critics argue that such
programs are ineffective and counterproductive.
Forced Speaking
  
Step 3 involves forced public support for SJF views, a
concept embodied by the mantra "silence is violence." This
coercive environment fosters a culture where dissent is not
tolerated and conformity is enforced. Examples from

companies and educational institutions highlight the
pressures placed on individuals to publicly align with SJF
ideologies.
Impact on Society
  
The chapter outlines how SJF has distorted the marketplace
of ideas, resulting in a breakdown of trust in institutions and
creating polarized environments where fear and silence
dominate discourse. The consequences extend to social
dynamics, leading to a reduction in open dialogue and critical
thinking.
Failure of Institutions
  
Urban critiques how institutions like the ACLU have shifted
focus away from free speech towards political activism,
losing their core mission. This trend exemplifies how the
foundations of liberal democracy are at risk as various
movements prioritize allegiance over individual rights.
The Cycle of Illiberalism
  
The relationship between the opposing political "golems"

(the Left and Right) exacerbates the problem, as each uses
the other's actions to justify their own illiberal behaviors. The
interplay between SJF and its detractors reinforces a toxic
cycle of rhetoric and intolerance.
Conclusion
  
Despite the grim narrative, Urban suggests that awareness
and courage are essential for countering SJF's influence.
Highlighting the potential for change, he advocates for open
dialogue and reestablishing the core values of liberalism,
reminding readers that societies must reclaim their
commitment to free speech and discourse to prevent
descending further into illiberalism.
