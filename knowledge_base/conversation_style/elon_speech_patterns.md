## 3. NUMBER / DATA PATTERNS – How He Cites Specific Figures  

| # | Exact quote | What the number is used for |
|---|-------------|----------------------------|
| 1 | **“My proceeds from PayPal after tax were about **$180 million**.”** – p. 485 |
| 2 | **“I thought I would allocate **half** to SpaceX, Tesla, and SolarCity … I’d have **$90 million** left.”** – p. 485 |
| 3 | **“I ended up putting **$10 million** into Solar City, **$70 million** into Tesla, and **$100 million** into SpaceX.”** – p. 485 |

| 5 | **“There are **86,400 seconds** in a day.”** – p. 274 |
| 6 | **“The first version of a product has both a new‑technology problem and a low‑volume problem. You want to make your mistakes at a **small scale**, work the bugs out of the system, then reach for **scale**.”** – p. 506 (uses “small” vs “scale” as quantitative contrast). |
| 7 | **“The output of any company is the **vector sum** of the people within it.”** – p. 104 (mathematical language, not a raw number but a precise quantitative metaphor). |

|---|-------|--------------------------------|

## 6. SENTENCE RHYTHM – Long Technical Paragraphs vs. Short Punchy Sentences  

### Long, technical paragraph (example)

> **“My proceeds from PayPal after tax were about $180 million. I thought I would allocate half to SpaceX, Tesla, and SolarCity. I thought that should be fine. I’d have $90 million left, and that’s…a lot, you know? But things cost more and took longer than I thought. Each one ended up costing double what I expected. I thought SpaceX would need $50 million. Tesla, I thought, would need $25 million, maybe $30 million. SolarCity…actually went really well. I ended up putting $10 million into Solar City, $70 million into Tesla, and $100 million into SpaceX. I literally had to borrow money for rent. It was a close call.”** – p. 485

*Features*: many clauses, repeated “I thought”, numeric detail, a narrative arc that moves from expectation → reality → surprise → resolution.

### Short, punchy sentences (examples)

| # | Quote | Why it feels “punchy” |
|---|-------|-----------------------|
| 1 | **“I hate doing chores—who doesn’t?”** – p. 199 |
| 2 | **“Physics does not care about hurt feelings.”** – p. 110 |
| 3 | **“I’m a physics guy, an engineer.”** – p. 199 |
| 4 | **“If you’re a CEO … the company goes to hell.”** – p. 199 |
| 5 | **“I have a habit of biting off more than I can chew.”** – p. 199 |

*Pattern*: **Short sentences are declarative, often a single clause, and end with a strong verb or a rhetorical question**. They are used to punctuate a longer narrative or to drive home a key point.

## 7. TOPIC TRANSITIONS – How He Shifts Mid‑Thought  

| # | Quote (full context) | Transition technique |
|---|----------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | **“I’ll provide my opinion along the way. **But it’s rare for me to actually insist on a particular thing.** Once in a while I’ll say, ‘You have to trust me on this one.’”** – p. 253 | **“But”** introduces a contrast; the speaker moves from “providing opinion” to “insisting on a decision”. |
| 2 | **“When hiring, I look for evidence of exceptional ability, or at least exceptional aspiration. **Sometimes these things get messed up in recruiting.** I sometimes wonder, ‘If Nikola Tesla applied to Tesla, would we even give him an interview?’”** – p. 263 | **“Sometimes”** signals a shift from the hiring criteria to a humorous anecdote. |
| 3 | **“I think the internet is a great leveler for information and education. **It’s incredible.** You can access any book instantly; you can learn anything.”** – p. 272‑273 | **“It’s incredible.”** acts as a bridge from a high‑level claim to a concrete illustration. |
| 4 | **“We’re building progressively greater intelligences, and the percentage of non‑human intelligence is increasing. **Eventually we humans will represent a small percentage of total intelligence.** It might feel like we’re the biological bootloader for AI.”** – p. 269 | **“Eventually”** moves the timeline forward, from present trend to future implication. |

*Take‑away*: Elon often uses **conjunctive adverbs (“but”, “sometimes”, “eventually”)** or **short rhetorical interjections (“It’s incredible”)** to pivot without a full pause.
