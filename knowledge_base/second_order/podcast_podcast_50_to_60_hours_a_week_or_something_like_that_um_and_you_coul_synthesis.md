## 4. HOW HE REASONED  

1. **First‑Principles Physics → Economics** – He treats **affordability** as a function of **energy per dollar** (battery cost per kWh) and **vehicle price**, then extrapolates demand as “infinite” once price hits a threshold.  
2. **Logistics Analogy** – “*Battles are won with tactics, wars with logistics*.” He equates **supply‑chain resilience** to **military supply lines**, stressing that a single broken node can cripple the whole operation.  
3. **Redundancy vs. Over‑capacity Trade‑off** – Uses the **three‑engine airplane** metaphor: redundancy is good, but **over‑capacity drives COGS up**. He quantifies this by referencing **CAPEX ratios** (Megapack vs. vehicle).  
4. **Probability‑Weighted Decision‑Making** – “We approach it probabilistically – what is the most likely thing to succeed?” He treats each engineering path as a **Monte‑Carlo trial**, discarding low‑probability branches early.  
5. **Scaling Numbers** – He repeatedly cites **orders of magnitude** (e.g., “increase neural‑net training capacity by an order of magnitude each year”) to anchor feasibility.  
6. **Historical Disruption Catalog** – He lists **tariffs, chip shortages, floods, fires** as a “log of known risk vectors,” then builds **dual‑source strategies** to hedge each.  
7. **Standardization as “Copy‑Paste”** – He frames factory design as **software engineering**: a **single codebase** (factory blueprint) that can be **forked** with minimal changes, reducing “engineering hours per new plant.”  
8. **Cost‑Savings via Direct‑Selling** – By eliminating **dealership margins** and **marketing spend**, he translates saved dollars directly into **consumer price reductions**.  

When pressed on push‑back (e.g., “Will AI help car manufacturing?”) he **deflects** to **risk‑aversion** (“I’m a little worried about AI”) rather than providing a technical answer, showing his habit of **defaulting to known constraints** when uncertainty is high.
