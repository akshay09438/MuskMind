## 4. HOW HE REASONED  

- **Physics‑first, data‑second:** He repeatedly frames arguments in terms of *energy, safety numbers, and hard‑data miles* (“billions of miles”, “50‑100 % safer”).  
- **Analogy to software:** Cars are “**vehicles literally and figuratively for autonomy**,” treating the hardware as a *platform* for a SaaS product.  
- **Historical precedent:** He cites the **first fatality** as a turning point for regulators (“the data around the first fatality swung in our favor”).  
- **Scaling logic:** Uses a **“march of nines”** metaphor for reliability (e.g., 99.999% uptime) to explain why the “last 1 %” is hard.  
- **Geographic variance:** Compares **US federal vs. California vs. Europe** to illustrate that regulatory risk is *jurisdiction‑specific* but overall “tiny delays.”  
- **Cost‑trajectory focus:** The 26 M‑car forecast hinges on **battery‑cost decline**, echoing his broader belief that *materials cost curves* drive market disruption.  
- **Risk‑reward hierarchy:** Prioritizes **highway autonomy** because *high‑speed accidents = higher value*; intersections are “harder” and thus later.  
- **Pushback handling:** When asked about regulators, he flips the argument: “**If you give us the data, they’ll understand**,” shifting the burden to the regulator.
