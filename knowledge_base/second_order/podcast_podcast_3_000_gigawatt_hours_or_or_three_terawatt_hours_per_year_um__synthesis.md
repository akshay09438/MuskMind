### 4. HOW HE REASONED  
- **First‑principles physics:**  He repeatedly reduces complex supply‑chain questions to elemental chemistry (“silicon is the most abundant element after oxygen”) and to simple energy‑density math (kWh per kg).  
- **Vivid analogies:**  *Silicon expansion* → “the cookie crumbles and gets gooey.”  *Cathodes* → “bookshelves where nickel, cobalt, manganese are the shelves and lithium is the book.”  *Structural battery* → “fuel‑tank‑in‑the‑wing of an aircraft.”  
- **Cost‑per‑kWh as the universal metric:**  Every material discussion ends with a dollar‑per‑kWh figure (e.g., “$1.20/kWh anode”, “15 % reduction in cathode $/kWh”).  
- **Scaling logic:**  He links factory size (“the size of that factory on the previous page”) directly to the ability to hit the 3 TWh target, arguing that *more* manufacturing automatically *reduces* cost.  
- **Handling pushback:**  When asked about feasibility, he leans on concrete milestones (“we have a pilot recycling plant next quarter”) and on the *inevitability* of physics (“if you look at the energy density, nickel wins”).  
- **Historical precedent:**  He cites aircraft design evolution (“fuel tanks moved from cargo to wing”) to justify the structural‑battery concept.
