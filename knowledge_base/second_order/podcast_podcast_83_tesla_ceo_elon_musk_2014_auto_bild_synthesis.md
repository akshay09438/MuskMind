---

### 1. Context & Setting  
- **When:** Late‑summer 2014, recorded for the German automotive magazine *Auto Bild* (podcast format).  
- **Interviewer:** Unnamed German journalist (the “host” in the transcript).  
- **Company backdrop:**  
  *Tesla* had just begun ramp‑up of the Model 3 “high‑volume” vehicle and the Gigafactory was under construction.  
  *SpaceX* was recovering from three consecutive launch failures (2008) and had just secured a $1.6 bn NASA contract (Dec 2008).  
  *SolarCity* was still a small subsidiary; Musk was still allocating a tiny slice of his time to it.  
  The interview captures Musk at a point where his personal work‑hours were still extreme (≈80‑85 h / wk) but beginning to plateau after the 2008‑09 crisis.

### 3. Direct Quotes (the most revealing)

1. **On work hours:** “These days it’s probably eighty‑eighty‑five hours a week… the difficulty in pain of work hours really increases exponentially, it’s not linear.”
2. **On Diet Coke & coffee:** “I’m trying to cut down… maybe one or two a day now… coffee I usually cut a two a day… I used to have so much coffee and Diet Coke that I got really wired.”
3. **On Tesla ownership:** “I certainly have a strong influence… but there are many people at the companies… it’s a big team effort from a lot of talented people.”
4. **On CEO tenure:** “I’m committed to be CEO through the high‑volume production vehicle… two more years at least, probably three or four… I will never leave Tesla, ever, but I may not be CEO forever.”
5. **On hiring philosophy:** “I really just ask them to tell me the story of their career… what are some of the tougher problems they dealt with… I’m looking for evidence of exceptional ability.”
6. **On education vs ability:** “There’s no need even to have a college degree… Bill Gates, Larry Ellison, Steve Jobs didn’t graduate… if you see that track record, it will likely continue.”
7. **On fear:** “I feel fear quite strongly, but I override the fear when the thing is important enough… it’s a drive‑override sphere.”
8. **On SpaceX risk:** “I thought both Tesla and SpaceX would fail at the beginning… I put half the money from PayPal into each.”
9. **On the 2008 crisis:** “That was the closest I’ve ever come to a nervous breakdown… the next morning NASA called… we barely scraped together enough for a fourth launch.”
10. **On moving to Silicon Valley:** “My best idea ever was coming to North America… California is very conducive to startup companies.”
11. **On future vision:** “I’d like to see humanity go beyond Earth and have people on Mars… widespread adoption of electric vehicles and renewable energy.”
12. **On Hyperloop:** “I think the Hyperloop is more likely to happen… Los Angeles to Las Vegas is a popular route.”
13. **On personal health:** “I work out once or twice a week… treadmill, some weights… I should get more often for sure.”
14. **On personal regret:** “I should have been CEO of Tesla from the beginning… that would have been the smart move.”
15. **On mortality:** “If you’re gonna die somewhere, why not on Mars? … I don’t really care what happens to my ashes.”

---

### 4. How He Reasoned  

- **Physics‑style scaling:** He treats work‑hour pain as *exponential*: “the difficulty… really increases exponentially, it’s not linear.”  
- **Historical analogues:** Cites **Bill Gates**, **Larry Ellison**, **Steve Jobs** to argue that *formal education is not a predictor of impact*.  
- **Numbers as anchors:** Uses concrete figures (e.g., “80‑85 h / wk”, “$40 k for a car”, “half the PayPal money”) to ground abstract ideas.  
- **Risk‑vs‑Reward calculus:** “If the stakes are high… we overcome the fear.” The 2008 crisis story shows a *binary* decision: *either die* or *push the last launch*.  
- **Team‑first framing:** Even when claiming influence, he immediately adds “it’s a big team effort,” reflecting a *systems‑thinking* bias.  
- **Counter‑factual “what‑ifs”:** He openly entertains “what if I had been CEO from day 1?” showing a habit of *post‑mortem* analysis.  
- **Geography as a lever:** “North America was the best idea” – he treats location as a *first‑order variable* for success.  
- **Emotion as a control knob:** Fear is *real* but *overridden*; he treats emotions like a *parameter* to be tuned rather than a fixed trait.

---

### 5. What This Reveals About How He Thinks  

- **Relentless drive + self‑awareness:** He can quantify his own *pain* and *addiction* (caffeine, Diet Coke) and actively work to reduce them.  
- **Truth‑seeking:** “I care a lot about the truth of things and trying to understand the truth of things.” He frames problem‑solving as a *search for objective reality*.  
- **Risk as a function of importance:** Fear is not a barrier; it’s a *signal* that he can consciously suppress when the mission matters.  
- **Talent over pedigree:** He values *demonstrated problem‑solving* more than credentials, reflecting a *first‑principles* approach.  
- **Long‑term vision vs short‑term pain:** Even while describing personal exhaustion, he constantly references *future milestones* (Mars, Hyperloop, Model 3).  
- **Iterative learning:** The 2008 narrative shows he treats failure as *data* (“budgeted for three failures, survived the fourth”).  
- **Humor & humility:** He admits to “whistling badly,” “not caring about ashes,” and “I’m not fearless,” showing a *self‑deprecating* side that balances the mythic CEO image.

### 7. The Most Elon Moment  

**The 2008 “closest to a nervous breakdown” anecdote**  

> “That was the closest I’ve ever come to a nervous breakdown… the next morning NASA called… we barely scraped together enough for a fourth launch.”

**Why it epitomises Elon:**  
- **High‑stakes pressure:** He describes an existential crisis (multiple rocket failures, Tesla financing collapse) in vivid, personal terms.  
- **First‑principles resilience:** He had *budgeted for three failures* and *planned a fourth*—a concrete, physics‑style contingency plan.  
- **Emotional honesty + strategic optimism:** He admits fear and sadness, yet immediately pivots to the *opportunity* (NASA contract) that saved both companies.  
- **Narrative of survival:** This story is the cornerstone myth that fuels his later “fail fast, iterate” mantra across all ventures.  

---

**In a nutshell:** The 2014 Auto Bild interview is a rare window into Musk’s *raw self‑assessment*: a work‑obsessed, caffeine‑driven engineer who treats fear as a controllable variable, values talent over pedigree, and constantly measures his actions against a long‑term vision of humanity’s future. The themes—exponential pain, team‑first influence, calculated risk, and relentless drive—are the same levers that have shaped Tesla’s production ramp, SpaceX’s launch cadence, and Musk’s broader ambition to remake transportation and colonise Mars.
