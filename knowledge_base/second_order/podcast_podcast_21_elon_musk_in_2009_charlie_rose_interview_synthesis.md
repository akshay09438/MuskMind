- **When?** Late‑2009 (recorded for *Charlie Rose*).  
- **Interviewer:** Charlie Rose, PBS journalist.  
- **Company status:**  
  * **SpaceX** – just achieved its first orbital launch (Falcon 1, Sep 2008) and was gearing up for the first Falcon 9 launch from Cape Canaveral (planned 2010).

  * **Personal split:** “roughly evenly between Tesla and SpaceX,” with SolarCity “requiring very little” attention.

### 3. DIRECT QUOTES (THE BEST ONES)  
1. “The three areas that I thought would most affect the future of humanity … were the internet, the transition to a sustainable energy and transportation sector, and space exploration.”  
2. “To do a sub‑orbital flight you need a terminal velocity of about Mach 3; to get to orbit you need Mach 25. The energy scales with the square of the velocity – sub‑orbital is about 1.5 % of the energy of orbit.”  
3. “Private sector is generally better at doing things than the government … but the government should fund basic research.”  
4. “The battery is the single most important thing … the difficulty is really at the pack level, not the cell level.”  
5. “The Tesla battery pack in the Roadster … weighs about a thousand pounds and has the energy content of two and a half gallons of gasoline.”  
6. “An electric motor is about ninety percent efficient at turning electricity into motion, versus ~18 % for a gasoline engine.”  
7. “We want to serve as a catalyst for accelerating the electric‑car revolution.”  
8. “The price of gas at the pump does not reflect the true cost of gasoline because we’re not paying for CO₂, wars, etc. It’s a subsidy.”  
9. “If SpaceX doesn’t do it we will be entirely dependent on the Russians to carry our astronauts … which I think is outrageous.”  
10. “New technology is expensive when it starts out … you have to make mistakes at a small scale.”  
11. “If you want a high‑performance car with a clean conscience this is the only option.”  
12. “I’ve been criticized for saying we’re pure‑electric … it’s not an attack on the Volt, it’s just the physics of efficiency.”  

---

### 4. HOW HE REASONED  
- **First‑principles physics:**  Mach numbers, energy ∝ v², efficiency percentages for motors vs ICEs.  
- **Quantitative scaling:**  “sub‑orbital … 9 units of energy, orbit … 625 units” – a concrete 1.5 % figure that makes the orbital challenge visceral.  
- **Historical analogies:**  Early computers and cell phones as “toys for rich people” that later mass‑produced; sports cars as a stepping‑stone to affordable EVs.  
- **Economic externalities:**  Gasoline price as a hidden subsidy; draws a parallel to over‑fishing.  
- **Risk‑cost analysis:**  “value of the thing you create > cost of inputs” – a cost‑benefit lens applied to both rockets and batteries.  
- **Handling pushback:**  When challenged on pure‑electric vs hybrid, he reframes the debate as “physics of efficiency,” not a personal attack.  
- **Strategic foresight:**  Links SpaceX’s orbital capability to national security (avoid Russian dependence) and Tesla’s scale‑up to “mass‑production as soon as possible.”  

---

### 5. WHAT THIS REVEALS ABOUT HOW HE THINKS  
- **Mission‑first, metric‑driven:**  Every statement is tied to a measurable impact (energy units, efficiency, production numbers).  
- **Optimism tempered by hard physics:**  He is willing to be bold (“we’ll launch Falcon 9”) but only after grounding the claim in physics.  
- **Long‑term externalities dominate his calculus:**  Climate, geopolitical, and market subsidies are the “real costs” he wants to internalize.  
- **Iterative, small‑scale learning:**  He treats early failures as inevitable and necessary, echoing his “make mistakes at a small scale” mantra.  
- **Competitive edge through private‑sector speed:**  Belief that “optimization” is the private sector’s natural advantage, driving his aggressive timelines.  
- **Personal risk management:**  Acknowledges a shift from youthful risk‑taking to a more measured approach now that he has families and multiple companies.

### 7. THE MOST ELON MOMENT  
**The sub‑orbital vs orbital energy comparison**  

> “To do a sub‑orbital flight you need a terminal velocity of about Mach 3 … to get to orbit you need Mach 25 … sub‑orbital is about 1.5 % of the energy of orbit.”

**Why it epitomises Elon:**  
- **Physics‑first framing:** He reduces a massive engineering challenge to a simple ratio, making the “impossible” feel quantifiable.  
- **Clear communication:** The analogy instantly conveys why orbital launch is a fundamentally different problem than sounding rockets.  
- **Strategic implication:** By exposing the energy gap, he justifies the massive capital and talent investment required for SpaceX, while also highlighting why “sub‑orbital” companies (e.g., Virgin Galactic) cannot substitute for true orbital capability.  
- **Confidence + humility:** He states the numbers with certainty, yet acknowledges the “very difficult technical challenge” and the tiny pool of rocketry talent, showing both boldness and realistic appraisal.
